● Gate-Interrupts for MoE LLMs — Critical Analysis & Architecture Plan

  1. Restating the proposal, precisely

  You're proposing an inference-time mechanism that lets a non-routed expert in
  a sparse-MoE LLM override or amend the routed expert's contribution when the
  non-routed expert produces a high-confidence signal that the input touches its
   domain in a critical way. A trainable "feedback/interrupt" layer learns when
  to fire.

  Before evaluating, I need to nail down what is actually being claimed vs.
  assumed, because a few of the assumptions matter for whether this is even
  well-posed:

  #: A1
  Assumption in the proposal: Experts cleanly specialize by semantic domain
    ("food expert", "vet expert")
  Reality (from literature): Partially true. Mixtral/DeepSeek studies show
    specialization is token-/syntax-level in shallow  layers, semantic/domain in
   
    deep layers, but never as  clean as a domain label. Many experts are
    polysemantic; only expert paths (sequences of routing decisions across
    layers) become quasi-monosemantic (Polysemantic Experts,  Monosemantic 
  Paths,
     Closer Look at MoE).
  Impact: The food/vet metaphor doesn't map onto a single expert; it maps onto a

    route across many layers. This complicates "which expert should interrupt."
  ────────────────────────────────────────
  #: A2
  Assumption in the proposal: A non-selected expert's logit confidence is a
    reliable signal of "important and critical" knowledge
  Reality (from literature): Neural confidence is famously poorly calibrated.
    Bayesian-MoE and gate-entropy work confirm this; the standard router is
    well-calibrated on easy tokens  but ~useless on the hard ones where 
    re-routing would  help most (Counterfactual Routing Analysis).
  Impact: The very tokens where you want the interrupt to fire are the tokens
    where the confidence signal is least trustworthy.
  ────────────────────────────────────────
  #: A3
  Assumption in the proposal: Non-routed experts could produce evaluable logits
    at inference
  Reality (from literature): Standard sparse MoE does not run non-routed experts
   
    at all — that's the entire efficiency point. You must either run them
  anyway,
     run cheap proxies for them, or change the architecture.
  Impact: This is the central design constraint.
  ────────────────────────────────────────
  #: A4
  Assumption in the proposal: A single interrupt point can correct a wrong
  gating
    decision
  Reality (from literature): Routing happens per-layer, per-token, often in
  24-60
     MoE layers. An "interrupt" is not a global event; it's a stack of per-layer

    decisions.
  Impact: Forces a design choice: per-layer interrupt, output-layer interrupt,
  or
    sequence-level interrupt. These are very different systems.

  Already we see the proposal needs sharpening on three axes: where in the 
  network the interrupt lives, what signal triggers it, and what compute it 
  costs.

  2. Prior art — does anyone do this already?

  Short answer: no one does exactly this, but four families of work circle 
  around it, and one of them (SteerMoE) is close enough that any paper here must
   carefully position against it.

  2.1 Directly adjacent

  - SteerMoE (2509.09660) — Identifies behavior-linked experts offline by
  comparing activation between paired safe/unsafe inputs, then manipulates 
  router logits at inference (sets target experts' scores to max+ε or min-ε).
  This is a gate override, and it improves safety +20% on red-team data and
  faithfulness +27% on RAG. Crucially: it's static — the experts to steer are 
  decided offline, not based on the runtime output of a non-routed expert. Your
  proposal is the dynamic, content-conditional version.
  - SAFEx (2506.17368) — Demonstrates that safety knowledge is localized: just
  12 experts (of 6,144) in Qwen3-30B-A3B control 22% of refusal behavior.
  Decomposes into HCDG (detection) and HRCG (response control) groups. This is
  the strongest empirical validation of your premise — the "vet expert" really
  does exist as a localized substructure, at least for safety.
  - Counterfactual Routing Analysis (2605.07260) — The single most important
  paper for your idea. On "fragile" hard tokens, the chosen expert is best only
  <1% of the time, and 20.4 percentage points of next-token probability is 
  recoverable purely by re-routing within a frozen model. So non-routed experts
  genuinely contain better answers on hard cases. This is the empirical headroom
   your interrupt would chase.

  2.2 Related machinery

  - Cascade / utility-driven speculative MoE (2506.20675) — Speculative decoding
   adapted to MoE; introduces "utility" gating between draft and verify. Not a
  non-routed-expert override, but shows that runtime arbitration between
  cheaper/coarser and expensive paths is tractable in MoE serving.
  - DeepSeekMoE shared experts — DeepSeek-V3 has 2 shared experts that always 
  run alongside 6 top-k routed experts (of 64). This is the precedent for "an
  expert that bypasses gating." Your interrupt expert can be implemented as a
  conditionally-loud shared expert.
  - Capsule networks / routing-by-agreement (Sabour et al., 2017) — Conceptual
  ancestor. Lower-level capsules vote for higher-level ones based on agreement;
  competition between capsules is the routing signal. Different domain, but the
  "non-elected unit can become dominant via signal strength" idea is the same
  skeleton.
  - Multi-agent debate / verifier-critic — Inference-time, prompt-level analog
  of what you want, but with whole-model agents rather than sub-experts. Shows
  the governance problem you'll inherit: clones converge to groupthink,
  structured rewriting attacks can amplify bad outputs by up to 180%
  (Multi-Agent Debate).

  2.3 Adversarial context you can't ignore

  A new gate signal is a new attack surface. GateBreaker (2512.21008) and the
  MoE Routing Safety Bypass database entry already show that fine-tuning can
  create "alignment shortcuts" through routing manipulation. Any interrupt
  mechanism introduces a new learnable component that can be poisoned to
  suppress interrupts on adversarial inputs. This must be part of the threat
  model from day one.

  Net of prior art: the premise (non-routed experts know things) is now
  empirically established. The mechanism you propose (dynamic,
  content-conditional interrupt) is a real open problem. SteerMoE is your
  closest neighbor; the contribution would be making it dynamic.

  3. Feasibility — my honest assessment

  Conditional yes, but probably not in the form you described. Three hard
  problems and one mostly-solved problem:

  3.1 The compute paradox (hard, but solvable)

  You cannot get logits from a non-routed expert without computing it. Three
  viable architectural responses, each with trade-offs:

  1. Always-on probe heads. Add a tiny (e.g., 1-2 layer MLP, < 1% expert params)
   "summary head" per expert that always runs on the input. The summary head
  outputs a low-dimensional vector + a scalar "this is in my critical
  wheelhouse" score. Cheap, but its faithfulness to the full expert's actual
  output is questionable — you're trusting a 1% surrogate to flag when the 100%
  expert would disagree.
  2. Speculative two-pass. Run the routed forward pass to produce a draft. In a
  separate cheap pass, evaluate the input against each expert's "domain
  detector" head (à la SAFEx's HCDG concept). If any non-routed expert flags
  critical relevance + a divergence from the draft → run the full non-routed
  expert and arbitrate. Trades latency for accuracy.
  3. Shared "critical-knowledge" experts. A small number of always-on experts
  trained specifically to carry safety-critical, factuality-critical, and 
  domain-critical knowledge that should never be silenced by routing. Borrowed
  from DeepSeek-V3. This is the safest, simplest version of your idea — and
  probably the strongest first paper.

  Of these, (3) is the lowest-risk way to deliver a publishable, useful version 
  of your idea in 2026, and (2) is the "true to the original vision" path. (1)
  is appealing but its faithfulness becomes an empirical question that may sink
  the whole architecture.

  3.2 The confidence signal is unreliable where you need it most (hard)

  The counterfactual routing paper is brutally specific: confidence is
  well-calibrated on easy tokens, where you don't need re-routing, and
  uninformative on fragile tokens, where you do. So raw logit margin / softmax 
  confidence is not a safe interrupt trigger in its naive form.

  Workable substitutes:
  - Trained interrupt classifier on (input, draft-output) pairs. Treat "should
  we interrupt?" as a learned binary classification problem with paired data
  showing critical interventions vs. spurious ones. This is closer to what you
  originally proposed and is the right framing.
  - Cross-expert disagreement (capsule-network style): interrupt when a
  non-routed expert's draft differs sharply from the routed one AND the
  non-routed expert has been trained to "claim ownership" of this token type.
  Requires training signal for ownership.
  - Calibrated uncertainty via Bayesian-MoE-style heads (2511.08968) for the
  interrupt scorer specifically.

  3.3 Training dynamics (very hard, biggest research risk)

  MoE training is already fragile: load-balancing aux loss conflicts with the LM
   objective, leading models like DeepSeek-V3 to drop aux loss entirely in favor
   of bias-based load balancing (2408.15664). Adding a third competing objective
   ("learn when to interrupt") creates:
  - Reward-hacking risk: the interrupt scorer can learn to fire constantly (free
   gradient on whichever expert it activates), or never (collapse to identity).
  You will need calibration loss + sparsity loss + safety-critical recall loss,
  each fighting the others.
  - Specialization disruption: if interrupts fire during training, routed
  experts get less signal on the tokens that were "interrupted away,"
  potentially weakening their own specialization. You may need a frozen-base, 
  interrupt-only fine-tune phase — similar to how SteerMoE never modifies expert
   weights.
  - No supervised data exists. You need a benchmark of (prompt, routed-answer, 
  critical-correction, ground-truth-which-expert-owned-the-correction). This
  dataset doesn't exist. Building it (synthetic + curated) is a multi-month
  sub-project on its own.

  3.4 Adversarial robustness (medium)

  Once you ship a trainable interrupt, the GateBreaker-style attack surface
  widens. Any new training objective that gates safety-critical behavior is a
  new fine-tuning bypass vector. Acceptable, but must be in the threat model and
   tested against existing MoE jailbreak benchmarks.

  Verdict

  Feasible as a research direction; not feasible as a drop-in retrofit to 
  existing Mixtral/DeepSeek checkpoints without significant retraining. A v1
  that proves the concept on a small, trained-from-scratch MoE (~1-2B active
  parameters) is realistic in a 3-6 month research cycle. A claim of beating
  Mixtral 8x22B / DeepSeek-V3-class models on safety+factuality benchmarks is a
  12-18 month effort.

  4. Proposed architecture (concrete)

  I'd recommend starting with a hybrid of approaches (2) and (3) above — they're
   complementary and let you ablate cleanly.

  4.1 Components

                  │  ┌──────────┐                        │

  The key trainable additions are: the per-expert probe heads (small MLPs), the
  critical-knowledge expert(s) (full expert size), and the interrupt scorer (a
  small attention/MLP over probe outputs).

  4.2 Where it lives in PyTorch

  Concretely, this is a modification of MixtralSparseMoeBlock (or DeepSeek's MoE
   block). The cleanest implementation path:
  - Fork HuggingFace transformers/models/mixtral/modeling_mixtral.py for the
  reference architecture, or use torchtune's MoE module (RFC #1902). Mixtral
  block is ~80 LOC; modification is ~150-200 LOC.
  - Build on top of megablocks for efficient sparse compute if you want training
   scale, but for v0 stay with the simple loop implementation — it's debuggable.
  - Custom layer order: probe heads run in parallel with expert dispatch;
  interrupt scorer must run after router so it sees the routed output to compute
   divergence.

  4.3 Training regime (3-phase)

  1. Phase 1: Base MoE pretrain (or use existing checkpoint). Standard MoE
  training, no interrupt mechanism. For research v0, train a small (~500M
  active, 8 experts) MoE from scratch on a curated corpus where you control
  domain mix. (Mixtral-class checkpoints are too expensive to retrain.)
  2. Phase 2: Critical-expert fine-tune. Freeze backbone, train the always-on
  critical-knowledge experts on a safety+factuality dataset. This isolates "what
   knowledge must never be missed."
  3. Phase 3: Interrupt scorer training. Freeze everything else. Train the probe
   heads + interrupt scorer on paired data: (prompt, routed-only-output, 
  critical-corrected-output). Objective: interrupt scorer outputs α≈1 when
  correction is needed, α≈0 otherwise. Add calibration loss + sparsity prior
  (interrupts should be rare).

  4.4 Evaluation

  - Headroom benchmark. Construct or adapt a benchmark where the gold answer
  requires knowledge that's localized to a non-routed expert (the "dog + berry
  pie" type case). Measure recovery rate vs. baseline.
  - No-regression suite. Standard MoE benchmarks (MMLU, GSM8K, HumanEval) should
   not drop. This is the load-bearing eval — if interrupts cause regressions on
  non-critical tokens, the system fails its bar.
  - Safety benchmark. Re-run SAFEx-style probes; HarmBench; AdvBench. Show
  interrupts catch refusal-bypass cases routed-experts miss.
  - Adversarial. GateBreaker / SteerMoE-unsafe steering. Show your interrupt
  mechanism is robust to routing-bypass attacks.
  - Calibration. ECE for the interrupt scorer; how often it fires vs. how often
  it should fire.

  5. Unknowns / open questions you'll have to resolve

  These are the things I cannot resolve from literature alone — they need
  experiments:

  1. How faithful are probe heads to full-expert behavior? Unknown. May need to
  compare probe-head verdict to full-expert forward on a calibration set.
  2. Does adding always-on critical experts change overall expert 
  specialization? The shared experts in DeepSeek absorb "universal" patterns;
  adding specialized shared experts may distort other experts' specialization.
  Open empirical question.
  3. Per-layer vs. terminal interrupt? I sketched per-layer. A terminal-only
  interrupt (act once at the unembedding) is much simpler but loses the
  depth-wise correction signal. Worth ablating.
  4. What's the right granularity for interrupt firing — per-token, per-span, 
  per-response? Per-token is the natural MoE granularity but may produce
  flickery outputs. Per-response requires a different control surface.
  5. Is the dataset of "critical interventions" buildable at sufficient scale?
  This may be the dataset-engineering blocker.
  6. Does this work without retraining experts? Strong if yes (you can retrofit
  existing models). SteerMoE shows static steering works without retraining;
  your dynamic version probably needs at least the probe heads trained.

  6. Where I think this should go

  If I were writing the research proposal, I'd structure it in two milestones
  with a decision gate between them:

  Milestone 1 (3 months): "Static critical experts as a retrofit."
  Take an open Mixtral / DeepSeek-MoE checkpoint, freeze it, add 1-2 always-on
  critical experts trained via LoRA on a safety+factuality corpus, and a fixed
  (non-trainable) interrupt rule based on probe-head outputs. Show measurable
  improvement on a constructed "non-routed-expert knows better" benchmark with
  no regression on MMLU. This is publishable on its own and de-risks the harder 
  problem.

  Decision gate: Did probe heads turn out to faithfully predict full-expert
  disagreement? If yes → proceed. If no → either rethink the trigger, or fall
  back to "static critical experts always mix in at fixed weight."

  Milestone 2 (6 months): "Trained dynamic interrupt scorer."
  Build the paired-correction dataset. Train the interrupt scorer end-to-end
  with calibration + sparsity + safety-recall losses. Compare against static
  baseline. This is the contribution.

  ---
  My opinion, summarized

  The proposal is theoretically sound, partially empirically supported, and 
  architecturally non-trivial but tractable. The strongest version is not
  "interrupts override the gate based on logit confidence" (the confidence
  signal is weakest where you need it most), but rather "a learned, calibrated 
  arbiter decides when always-on critical-knowledge experts should partially 
  override the routed mixture, based on input-conditioned probes and 
  routed-output divergence." Framed that way, it has a clear publishable
  contribution against SteerMoE (dynamic vs. static), a clear connection to
  SAFEx (operationalizes their finding), and concrete experimental headroom (the
   20pp recoverable on fragile tokens from counterfactual routing).

  The biggest single risk is dataset construction for training the interrupt
  scorer. The biggest single technical risk is probe-head faithfulness. Either
  of those failing turns this into "an interesting negative result" rather than
  "a new architecture."
